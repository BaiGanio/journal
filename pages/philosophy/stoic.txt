The most famous Stoic philosophers include Marcus Aurelius (the emperor), Seneca (the advisor/playwright), and Epictetus (the former slave), who together represent the practical, Roman-era application of the philosophy. They emphasized virtue, resilience, and controlling one's own reactions to live a fulfilling life.

Key Historical Stoics
Zeno of Citium (c. 301 BC): Founder of the Stoic school in Athens, setting the foundation for living in accordance with nature.
Chrysippus of Soli: The third head of the school known for formalizing Stoic logic, often called the "second founder" of Stoicism.
Cleanthes of Assos: Second leader of the school, best known for his "Hymn to Zeus".
Seneca the Younger: A wealthy Roman statesman and advisor to Nero, known for his letters on practical ethics and overcoming adversity.
Epictetus: Born a slave, he taught that personal freedom comes from controlling one's own mind, emphasizing the "dichotomy of control".
Marcus Aurelius: Roman Emperor who recorded his personal Stoic reflections in Meditations while leading the empire.
Cato the Younger: A Roman senator and political hero known for his unyielding commitment to virtue and opposition to Julius Caesar.
