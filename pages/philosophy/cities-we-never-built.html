Cities We Never Built

The utopian urbanism of science fiction and what it reveals about us

ARTICLE_IMAGE_CAPTION_1

A century ago, the future was bright, metallic, and full of promise. We imagined cities that spiraled into the sky, monorails gliding like silver needles, robots serving breakfast, and clean energy humming beneath glass streets. The future was not a threat — it was a destination.

History has a strange sense of humor.

Today, we live in a world that is undeniably advanced, yet somehow less futuristic than the one our grandparents were promised.
We have supercomputers in our pockets, but our cities still drown in traffic. We have AI that can write poetry, but we still queue at government offices.
We have quantum processors, but our urban spaces feel more like patched‑up relics than the gleaming utopias of Asimov, Le Guin, or Flash Gordon.

This is an essay about the cities we never built — and what their absence reveals about us.

The Blueprint of Desire

Science fiction has always been less about predicting the future and more about revealing our desires.

Asimov's Trantor — a planet-wide city of metal domes and endless corridors — was not a forecast. It was a fantasy of order, rationality, and mastery over chaos. A world where everything was engineered, optimized, controlled.
Le Guin's Anarres - by contrast, was a city built on scarcity, cooperation, and radical freedom. No skyscrapers, no neon, no chrome — just a society trying to live without domination.

Two cities. Two futures. Two philosophies.

And neither came to pass.

But they mattered because they told us what we wanted — or what we feared.

Cities are not just architecture. They are psychology poured into concrete. They are collective dreams made visible. And when we imagine future cities, we are really imagining the kind of people we hope to become.

The Century of Fairs and Futures

If you want to understand how the 20th century imagined the future, you don't go to books — you go to the World Fairs.

Paris 1900 gave us moving sidewalks and electric lighting.
New York 1939 promised "The World of Tomorrow" with highways in the sky and automated kitchens.
Osaka 1970showcased space colonies, humanoid robots, and the first hints of a digital society.
Seville 1992 imagined a world connected by networks, satellites, and global communication.

These fairs were not exhibitions. They were prophecies.

They told ordinary people:

This is what your life will look like. This is the city your children will inherit.

And people believed them.

The fairs were optimistic in a way that feels almost alien today. They assumed progress was inevitable, technology was benevolent, and the future would be cleaner, faster, brighter.

Flash Gordon flew across the screen with ray guns and rocket belts. Buck Rogers lived in a world of domed cities and anti‑gravity highways. Even dystopias of the era — like Metropolis — were visually stunning, as if the future could be terrifying but never boring.

The society were promised something spectacular.

The Future We Actually Built

And then… we built something else.

Our cities grew, but sideways, not upward.
Our transportation improved, but incrementally, not radically.
Our technologies exploded, but mostly in the invisible realm — software, data, networks.

The future arrived quietly, not with monorails but with microchips.

We got:

Smartphones instead of flying cars
Social networks instead of robot butlers
Algorithmic feeds instead of domed cities
Gig economies instead of universal automation
Data centers instead of gleaming spaceports

The irony is almost poetic:

We built the future of information, not the future of infrastructure.

The cities we inhabit today are not the cities of science fiction. They are the cities of capitalism, bureaucracy, and historical inertia. They are shaped by zoning laws, not imagination.

We built the internet, but not Trantor.
We built AI, but not Anarres.
We built convenience, but not wonder.

Quantum Cities and the Collapse of Possibility

Quantum physics teaches us that reality is not a single line but a cloud of possibilities.
A city is the same. It is a superposition of futures — until politics, economics, and human nature collapse it into one.

In the 20th century, the wave function of the future was wide. We could imagine anything.

But somewhere along the way, the wave function narrowed.
Not because technology failed — but because imagination did.

We learned to optimize instead of dream.
We learned to iterate instead of invent.
We learned to fear disruption instead of embrace it.

The quantum city — the city of infinite possible futures — collapsed into the city of good enough.

And so we live in a world where our phones feel futuristic, but our streets feel ancient. Where our algorithms are advanced, but our public spaces are neglected. Where our digital lives are sleek, but our physical lives are patched together.

The future shrank.

AI: The Mirror We Didn't Expect

Artificial intelligence was supposed to be the crown jewel of the future city.
- Robots sweeping the streets.
- Autonomous taxis gliding through neon avenues.
- Machine minds coordinating energy, traffic, climate, and communication.

Instead, AI became something stranger:
a mirror.

It reflects our biases, our desires, our fears, our habits. It amplifies what we already are. In a way, AI is the most honest technology we have ever built.

It does not pretend. It does not moralize. It simply learns from us — and becomes us.

Which raises a difficult question:

If AI is a mirror, and we don't like what we see…
...is the problem the machine, or the model it was trained on — us?

The cities of science fiction assumed that future humans would be wiser, calmer, more unified. But AI reveals that we are still chaotic, tribal, distracted, anxious.

We built a machine to manage the future, but the machine is telling us something uncomfortable:

You cannot build Trantor with the psychology of Twitter.
You cannot build Anarres with the economics of surveillance capitalism.
You cannot build utopia with the incentives of the present.

The Lost Future and the Brave New One

So what do we do with the cities we never built?

We could mourn them or we could shrug and say "That was naive".
We could blame politics, economics, or human nature.

But there is another option.We can treat those imagined cities the way Marcus Aurelius treated his meditations — not as predictions, but as reminders.

- Trantor reminds us to think big.
- Anarres reminds us to think ethically.
- Flash Gordon reminds us to think playfully.
- The World Fairs remind us to think optimistically.

The future is not a prophecy. It is a responsibility. And maybe the reason we never built those cities is simple:

We were waiting for the tools.

Now we have them.

- AI that can design entire districts and quantum computers that can simulate complex systems.
- Renewable energy that can power mega-cities and robotics that can build faster than humans.
- Networks that connect billions instantly.

The ingredients of the future exist. What we lack is the courage to assemble them.

The City as a Moral Choice

Every city is a moral document. It reveals what a society values.

Do we value speed or community?
Efficiency or beauty?
Profit or dignity?
Surveillance or trust?
Convenience or meaning?

The cities of science fiction were not just technological fantasies. They were ethical experiments.

Trantor asked:

What happens when knowledge becomes the foundation of civilization?

Anarres asked:

What happens when freedom becomes the foundation of civilization?

Our cities ask:

What happens when convenience becomes the foundation of civilization?

The answer is visible everywhere:

We have cities that are functional but uninspired. Connected but lonely. Efficient but fragile.

We get a world optimized for comfort, not purpose.

Returning to the Future We Lost

The future we were supposed to have — the one imagined in the 20th century — was not childish. It was ambitious. It believed in progress. It believed in humanity. It believed that tomorrow could be better than today.

Maybe it's time to believe that again.

Not in flying cars or ray guns. Not in chrome skyscrapers or domed cities. But in the idea that the future is something we build, not something we inherit.

The cities we never built are not failures.
They are invitations:

To imagine boldly. To design ethically. To build courageously. To dream collectively.

Because the truth is simple:

The future didn't disappear. We just stopped looking for it.

And maybe — just maybe — it's time to look again.
