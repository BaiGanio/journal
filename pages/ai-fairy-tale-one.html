AI fairy tale #1

Shadows, simulations, false prophets, and who controls the projector

Ancient New Zealand Caves Are Filled With Bioluminescence Glowworms

Somewhere around 380 BC, Plato sat down and described a group of prisoners chained inside a cave, facing a wall. Behind them, a fire burns. Between the fire and the prisoners, puppeteers carry objects — and the prisoners watch only the shadows those objects cast.
- They name the shadows.
- They argue about the shadows.
- They build entire governments around the shadows.

They have never once turned around.

What Plato could not have imagined — and honestly, I do not blame him for this — is that two and a half millennia later, we are going to solve this problem not by simply give up and turning around... But by building a better shadows projector and charging a monthly subscription for it. He-he ;/

The World of Ideas, Now Hosted on AWS, GCP, Azure... whatever

Plato's deeper argument was not just about the cave. It was about what is real. Behind the physical world — behind the shadows, behind the stones and trees and faces — he believed there existed a higher realm: the World of Forms. Perfect, eternal, unchanging archetypes. The perfect Circle. The perfect Justice. The perfect Horse. Every real dog and cat and human being you have ever seen is just a blurry, temporary copy.

You can laugh. But bare with me.

Because here is what modern physics quietly whispers back to Plato across the millennia: you might not be entirely wrong, old man.

Quantum mechanics tells us that at the base of reality, particles do not have definite properties until they are observed. Before the measurement, an electron exists in superposition — all possible states simultaneously, described by a probability wave. The moment you look, it collapses into one. The act of observation — of paying attention — participates in constructing reality.

Which is either profound or deeply unsettling, depending on how much coffee you have had.

"The universe is not only queerer than we suppose, but queerer than we can suppose."
J.B.S. Haldane

Now enter AI. A large language model is trained on the shadows — on billions of words that humans wrote about the world. Not the world itself. The words about the world. The representations. The descriptions. The political speeches, the newspaper articles, the Reddit arguments at 2am. The shadows on the wall, transcribed.

All this living in a servers, racks, datacenters... in the CLOUD. Consuming resources while describing the reality with our own words?!?! Replacing humans in a workflows it does not understand. Making stupid people rich, the rest - hungry.

And yet from this, something emerges that recognizes patterns, generates meaning, answers questions, writes poetry. Something that behaves, in many situations, like it knows things.

Plato would be fascinated, I guess. He would also immediately ask who controls the fire?

The Puppeteers Have a Board of Directors

Here is where the cave metaphor gets genuinely useful and genuinely dark at the same time.

In the original story, the puppeteers are unnamed. They carry objects. They cast shadows. They are simply there, behind the prisoners, as if the structure of the illusion has no author. Plato was being polite.

Today's puppeteers have names, valuations, and quarterly earnings calls.

The shadows you see — the news feeds, the recommendations, the AI-generated summaries of the world — are not neutral. They are shaped by systems optimized for engagement, for retention, for the specific kind of outrage that makes people click one more time before bed. The fire behind the wall is not burning for your enlightenment. It is burning for your attention, which is then sold.

This is not a conspiracy. It is just an incentive structure, which is somehow more frightening. Nobody decided to make the world worse. The cave built itself, one A/B test at a time.

On Wars, Hunger, and Very Confident Shadows

Consider the following: there is enough food produced on Earth right now to feed every living person. The mathematics are not close — we produce roughly 1.5 times what we need. And yet somewhere between 700 and 800 million people go to sleep hungry tonight. This is not a supply problem. It is a distribution problem, a political problem, a priorities problem.

We know this. It is not obscure information.

And yet on the wall of the cave — on the screen — what do we watch? We watch shadow-conflicts between shadow-factions, narrated by shadow-experts, in formats optimized to produce shadow-opinions that we then defend with great passion to other people watching the same wall in different rooms.

Wars are real. The bombs are real. The bodies are undeniably, tragically real. But our understanding of why they happen, who benefits, and what would actually stop them — that understanding is almost entirely constructed from shadows. From official statements, from embedded journalists, from the governments that fund the think tanks that advise the journalists.

The prisoners debate the shadows with tremendous sophistication. They have developed a whole vocabulary for the shadows. They give awards to the best shadow-explainers. They elect people based on which shadow most closely resembles what they fear or want.

Plato saw this. He just could not have predicted that we would then build machines to generate the shadows more efficiently.

The Quantum Twist Nobody Asked For

Here is where it gets strange — and I promise this is relevant and not just me spiraling at 1am.

If quantum mechanics is right that observation participates in reality, and if AI systems are now observing — processing, classifying, predicting — at a scale no human mind can match, then what happens to the wave function of our shared world?

This is not meant literally. Quantum effects do not scale to the macro world the way fever dreams suggest. But the philosophical question is not entirely unserious: if the models we build encode our biases, our patterns, our historical blind spots — and those models then shape what information reaches us, what gets amplified, what gets filtered — are we not, in some real sense, collapsing possibilities? Locking in a version of reality? Building a cave inside the cave?

Plato's philosopher escaped the cave, climbed toward the light, saw the actual sun, and returned to tell the others. They did not believe him. According to Plato, they killed him. (This was based on a real person. The real person was made to drink hemlock. Philosophy has always had a mortality problem.)

The modern equivalent of the philosopher returning from the light is someone publishing a correction to a viral story. They get three hundred views. The original shadow gets forty million.

And Yet — the World of Ideas Persists

This is the part where I refuse to end in despair, because Plato didn't either.

The World of Forms — the real, the true, the good — exists, for Plato, independently of whether anyone is looking at it. Justice is not less real because most governments ignore it. The perfect Circle does not become imperfect because our geometry teachers drew it with a shaky hand. The ideas persist.

And here is the quietly radical thing about that: it means the shadows do not get the final word.

AI, for all its astonishing capacity to generate plausible-sounding shadows, cannot invent a new truth. It can hallucinate. It can confabulate. It can produce extraordinarily convincing shadows of reasoning. But it cannot manufacture the thing that good reasoning actually points to — reality, in its stubborn, observer-resistant, physical-and-moral fullness.

People still go hungry whether the feed mentions it or not. Wars still kill people who had names and plans and morning routines. Climate shifts whether the algorithm promotes it or buries it.

Plato's Forms are immune to content moderation.

"Until philosophers are kings, or the kings and princes of this world have the spirit and power of philosophy... cities will never have rest from their evils."
Plato, The Republic

He wrote that expecting it to happen eventually. He had, in this one respect, more optimism than I do on a Tuesday.

But the point stands, extended forward: until the people who build the fire understand what the fire is for — not engagement, not retention, not quarterly growth — the prisoners will keep arguing about the shadows. And the hungry will stay hungry. And the wars will keep finding excellent justifications on page one.

The cave is not inevitable. Somebody built it. Somebody maintains it. And somebody, every generation, turns around.

The question is whether, this time, they are allowed to speak before the hemlock is poured.
