The Stoic and the Machine

What ancient philosophy teaches us about living with AI

15 January 2025 8 min read

Replace with your image caption · Source attribution if needed

Marcus Aurelius never had to worry about algorithmic feeds or notification anxiety. He ruled an empire, fought wars on the Danube, and still found time each morning to write private meditations that were never intended for publication. Yet those meditations — scribbled in Greek, in a military tent, nearly two thousand years ago — feel startlingly prescient the moment you set them beside a smartphone.

The Stoics built their entire philosophy around a single distinction: what is in our control, and what is not. Everything that happens to us from the outside — weather, other people's opinions, the outcome of an election, the recommendations of an algorithm — lies beyond the fence of our will. Everything that happens inside us — our judgments, our intentions, how we choose to respond — lies within it. Happiness, the Stoics argued, depends on learning to care only about what is inside the fence.

A Framework for Any Age of Disruption

When I first read Epictetus seriously, I was struck not by the philosophy's difficulty but by its structural elegance. It does not ask you to predict the future. It does not ask you to understand every force acting upon you. It only asks you to sort your experiences into two columns: mine, not mine. That is a framework you can apply to almost anything — including technologies that did not exist when the framework was designed.

"Men are disturbed not by the things which happen, but by the opinions about the things." — Epictetus, Enchiridion

Consider how we actually experience an AI-powered recommendation engine. The algorithm operates entirely outside our control. It ingests signals we barely know we're emitting — how long we hovered over an image, whether we scrolled past a headline slowly or quickly — and uses them to shape what we see next. The Stoic response is not to pretend the algorithm doesn't exist, and not to rage against it. It is to notice the one thing that remains stubbornly ours: the choice of whether to follow the recommendation.

The Inner Citadel and the Outer Feed

Marcus Aurelius called it the hegemonikon — the ruling faculty, the inner citadel. Modern neuroscience would call it executive function. Whatever we call it, it is the part of us capable of pausing between stimulus and response. Every notification, every algorithmically curated headline, every AI-generated reply is a stimulus. The citadel is what decides whether we react or respond.

The Stoics were not naive. They understood that the external world exerts genuine pressure. Epictetus was a slave; he knew what it meant to have almost nothing within the fence of his will. His point was not that the fence is wide. It is that even a small territory, zealously tended, is enough to live well within.

What We Owe the Machine

There is one place where I think the Stoic framework needs updating, and it is the question of collective responsibility. The Stoics were largely individualists. Aurelius had duties as emperor, but Stoic ethics is mostly about the private governance of the self. AI is not a private phenomenon. The models are trained on collective output; their effects are distributed across millions of lives.

To live well with AI, then, may require something the Stoics did not fully theorise: a civic Stoicism. One that asks not only what I can control in my private life, but what we can govern, together, in the design of the systems we build. The fence of the will may need to be a larger enclosure than Epictetus imagined.

And yet — when I close the browser, silence the phone, and sit with the morning coffee — it is still Aurelius I reach for. You have power over your mind, not outside events. Realize this, and you will find strength. Two millennia of noise, and the sentence still holds.
